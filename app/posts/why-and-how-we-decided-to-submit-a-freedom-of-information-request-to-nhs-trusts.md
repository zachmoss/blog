In May this year I went along to observe a hearing at the Court of Protection. It‚Äôs something [we at Compassion in Dying do sometimes](https://openjusticecourtofprotection.org/2022/06/23/she-is-religious-and-she-is-a-fighter-three-perspectives-on-best-interests-decision-making-in-the-court-of-protection-from-compassion-in-dying/) as the decisions made by this court are relevant to the people we support.

The court deals with a wide range of matters, and most cases are not relevant to the work we do. But generally a case is *relevant* to our work if it‚Äôs about whether:

1. the NHS should withdraw medical treatment, or 
2. a serious medical treatment decision is in a person's best interests

Sometimes there are very specific cases about an [advance decision to refuse treatment (ADRT)](https://compassionindying.org.uk/how-we-can-help/living-will-advance-decision/) or [lasting power of attorney (LPA)](https://compassionindying.org.uk/how-we-can-help/lasting-power-attorney-health-welfare/) which may also be relevant.

I‚Äôve recently been thinking about how we spend our time observing, analysing and using learnings from these cases... but that‚Äôs a separate blog post.

## Why this hearing prompted an FOI request
The particular case I observed was about someone who‚Äôd made an ADRT, and specifically whether it was ‚Äòvalid‚Äô and ‚Äòapplicable‚Äô to their situation. The [full judgment](https://caselaw.nationalarchives.gov.uk/ewcop/t3/2025/20) or a [blog post about the case](https://openjusticecourtofprotection.org/2025/05/05/determining-the-legal-status-of-a-living-will-personal-reflections-on-a-case-before-poole-j/) are worth reading if you have time as they set out the wider picture and context of the situation.

To summarise what was a complex case, there were some doubts about whether the person‚Äôs ADRT was valid and applicable to the situation they were in. The doubt came from multiple parties - but most relevant for us was that the NHS Trust who were caring for the person at the time questioned the ADRT. It transpired that there shouldn‚Äôt have been any doubt at all really but that even so, the matter should‚Äôve been referred to the court much earlier than it was.

We know (from our helpline service) that people often have problems in hospitals when it comes to having ADRTs or LPAs accepted or followed by healthcare professionals. We have some idea of why this might happen but there is a knowledge gap in terms of knowing specifically what‚Äôs going wrong. 

As the case was concluding, the NHS Trust implied their guidance on ‚ÄòMental Capacity Act Advance Decisions and Advance Statements‚Äô was at fault. They even submitted the particular policy and highlighted sections which they were going to update and improve as a result of the issues highlighted in the case:

Having had a chat with a colleague about the feasibility of submitting a [freedom of information (FOI) request](https://www.whatdotheyknow.com/help/about_foi) we decided as a team it was worth doing.

I think a key part of this decision was not committing up front to lots of follow-on work. We thought about it more as a ‚Äò*let‚Äôs try and see what we get*‚Äô idea.

## How we submitted our FOI
I‚Äôm not a policy person, so had to lean on my policy expert colleagues on how to submit the FOI.

Given there are over 200 NHS Trusts (of varying descriptions) we decided to use [WhatDoTheyKnow Pro](https://www.whatdotheyknow.com/pro) to submit a batch FOI request.

  <img src="/images/blot-wdtk.png" alt="Screenshot of the WhatDoTheyKnow Pro dashboard">

This handy tool takes some load of the initial request work by submitting them all in a big batch to 135 NHS Foundation Trusts for example. It also manages the email back and forth with the NHS Trust information governance team which saves your email inbox from a bit of mess. So far, I think it‚Äôs a good tool and worth the ¬£20/month cost.

Once we‚Äôd decided on the tool, we decided on submitting our FOI to the following NHS Trusts (or Health Boards):

- 14 NHS Scotland Healthboards
- 8 NHS Wales local Healthboards (includes 1 Trust)
- 135 NHS Foundation Trusts
- 57 NHS Hospital Trusts

We then decided on the FOI questions we‚Äôd ask. The below were what we thought would reveal the most useful information:

1. Does the Trust have a policy which outlines responsibilities of healthcare professionals towards patients who have an advance decision to refuse treatment or lasting power of attorney?
2. If there‚Äôs a dispute does the Trust have a process or course of action the person should follow?
3. Does the Trust have someone responsible for overseeing compliance with the Mental Capacity Act?

## Some early findings from 170ish NHS Trust policies
These findings are by no means final or complete as we‚Äôve been sent at least 170ish PDF policy documents so far. Most of them are 15 - 20 pages long. As a result we‚Äôve not done any sort of substantive analysis or documenting of what we‚Äôve been sent.

### Inconsistent policy naming
One of the quickest things I noticed was the inconsistencies with the names of policies we were being sent.

The most common type of policy was some form of policy for ‚Äòcomplying with the Mental Capacity Act‚Äô. But even here there were differences - some for example included [Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards (DOLS)](https://www.scie.org.uk/mca/dols/at-a-glance/) and others had separate policies.

Some NHS Trusts had specific ADRT policies in addition to a broader Mental Capacity Act compliance policy. Where this was the case, we don‚Äôt have an answer as to why they have this... just that they do. For our purposes it‚Äôs probably the case that a Trust which has a dedicated policy on ADRTs is more likely to have good practice than one which doesn‚Äôt... but that could be a totally wrong hypothesis!

### Different NHS staff policy authors
One very interesting finding is the differences between responsible NHS staff / authors of these policies.

It is not an unreasonable assumption to make sure an NHS Trust is following a specific law would suggest a specific named role would be needed to monitor compliance. And yet, look at this small selection of role titles of policy authors:

- Associate Director of Nursing for Safeguarding
- Head of Mental Health Law
- Associate Medical Director
- Safeguarding and Vulnerable Adult Lead Nurse
- Mental Health Act Manager
- Mental Capacity Act Lead 
- Head of Legal Services
- Head of Safeguarding 
- Clinical Lead End of Life Care

There is a quite a lot to unpick here, but it‚Äôs already useful in the sense that it gives our team a much more targeted way of escalating issues on behalf of our service users.

## What we plan to do now
In the workshop I mentioned we spent the biggest portion of the day discussing ideas of how we might use the results of our FOI.

Going back to the original idea, I stressed the importance of not committing up front to lots of follow-on work.

That being said, I wanted to give the team freedom to think up ideas about: 

1. What *could* be done
2. How it might help the people we support
3. How it might support our organisational policy goals
3. How much work it *might* take for their ideas

It was a slightly rushed part of the day due to me being late for my own workshop... but here are the ideas we came up with as a team:

- Use the policies in our professional education and training work.
- Use the positive examples (of NHS Trust policies) to influence the ‚Äòless good‚Äô ones.
- Submit a similar FOI to primary care providers.
- Use this work as a showcase of our policy expertise - on social media, blog posts.
- Do some follow-up work with clinicians on behaviour change - and balance this with FOI results to see what most influences behaviours. Good policy ‚â† Good practice.
- Contact (and help) the NHS Trusts with the worst policies to try and pre-empt problems for the people we support.
- Write a blog post about the work üòé.
- Conduct a full analysis of the policies and create some sort of ranking or coding of them to help staff use them.
- Create a searchable database of the policies for staff to use.
- Submit follow-up FOI to find out NHS Trust approaches to specific training on their policy.
- Pick 5 NHS Trusts to develop a relationship with and do follow-up work with because: Having policy AND relationship is probably better than JUST having the policy.

I like this list. There is a bit of crossover between a few of the ideas and we didn‚Äôt do as much exploration or fleshing out of details as I wanted. There also wasn‚Äôt much exploration of the work involved in each idea - maybe that was a bit ambitious, but I do think it‚Äôs a critical part of the next bit as we are a small team constantly struggling to deliver.
